Founded in 1749 by the British and used primary as a military base, though the Mi'kmaq having been there for thousands of years, Halifax's history is rich in marine, military, and commercial efforts. In 1755 the British deported the descendants of French settlers, the Acadians, rippling the end of the French people in the region. Over time Halifax grew into a booming trading post, but not without struggle as Halifax's location made it difficult for ports and land merchants to sell effectively. Fast forward to after the Napoleonic Wars (1793-1815), the mid 1800s became Halifax's golden years, the effects of the war resulting in many buying limber, Halifax's excess made for great economic times.

Soon railways made their way to the region and industrialism was afoot, Halifax and Dartmouth (a small region outside Halifax) started producing cotton, sugar, rope, and railcars. Although some were either damaged or destroyed in the Halifax explosion, 1917, where 2 ships collided, one housing explosives coming to port preparing for WW1. Moving into wartime activity (1914-18 and 1940-45) Halifax's location became beneficial for those in residence, as the city became a major port for shipping supplies to Europe. Since the 1950s, Halifax has become a busy city, continuing to grow into the 2000th century from a post-war naval hub into a modern center marked by urban renewal. The 1996 amalgamation unified surrounding communities into the Halifax Regional Municipality, shaping the city's identity we now know today.
